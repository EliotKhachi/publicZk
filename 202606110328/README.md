# Inspired by Marty Cagan

## Part 1: Lessons
### Biggest Flaws
-- Following a waterfall approach of ideas --> biz case --> roadmap --> requirements --> design --> build, test, deploy  
- Clear separation of responsibility between product, design, and engineering  
- Validates customer need way too late   
- Engineering is brought in way too late  
- Assuming they have good product ideas

**What good teams do**  
- assume half their ideas wont work
- de-risk early  

### Core Principles
- Tackle risks up front before deciding to build anything  
- Products are defined and designed collaboratively, not sequentially  
- It's all about solving problems, not implementing features. Roadmaps are not about output of features. They're about implementing a solution-- business results.  

### Key Concepts
**Holistic Product**
Think holistically about product. Not just in terms of features/functionality, but the tech that enables it, UX, how we monetize them, how we attract and win customers.  

**Continuous product and delivery**
Product teams do 2 things: 1) Discover the product to be built, and 2) deliver that product to market  

- Product Discovery means getting answers to 4 critical questions
    - Will the user buy this (or choose to use it)?
    - Can the user figure out how to use this?  
    - Can our engineers build this?  
    - Can our stakeholders support this?  
- Run lots of quick experiments, prototypes, for learning; NOT production-ready  
- Delivery means the necessary scale, performance, reliability, fault tolerance, security, privacy, internationalization, and localization, and the product works as advertised.  
**Product Vision**
Have a 2-10 year long-term objective of the product. Use prototypes to discover what that is, and build and release products in hopes of hitting PMF and achieving that vision.

## Part 2: People
MOST IMPORTANT PART OF THE BOOK

## Team
The best product teams are...
- a team of missionaries, all aligned on the vision, not a team of mercenaries who are told what to do.  
- have a critical mass of 1 product manager, 1 designer, and 2 engineers. Upper limit is about 8-12 engineers  
- a flat hierarchy. The product manager isn't the "boss"  
- in-person, full-time employees, and are very well acquainted with each other  
- fully understand business objectives and context, and are given the autonomy to feel ownership and be responsible for *outcomes*  
- don't rest after something is shipped out the door. They ensure it's working for the users and the business  

## Product Manager
**Product managers are responsible for evaluating opportunities and determining what gets built and delivered to customers.**

This is TABLE-STAKES to be a successful product manager. You can't skip ANY of them:  
- have the passion for the product  
- be enthusiastic about upcoming technologies and how to use them
- have deep knowledge of your customer, the data, your business and its stakeholders, and of your market and industry  
- the credibility with key executives
- the respect of their product team  
- be THE expert on the product
- be smart, creative and persistent  

Normally takes 2-3 months to get a new product manager up to speed. 
It's a ton of work. You need to seriously be on-board, work long hours, and prepare.

*When a product succeeds, it's because everyone on the team did what they needed to do. But when a product fails, it's the product manager's fault.*  

## Product Designer
"It's amazing to me how many companies I encounter that just don't understand why having strong and talented designers is so important."  

Problems due to the absence of a designer:  
- You do design: give engineers wireframes and they cobble together visual design themselves
- You don't do design: give engineers user stories and they're forced to work out the design  
- You do half the design: create wireframes then hand it off to a visual or graphic designer  

"In strong teams, the design informs the functionality as much as the functionality drives the design."  

Designers and product managers discover product solutions together. Fight the temptation to give them your own design ideas. Give them freedom to craft the solution. Include them on user/customer calls and interactions. Have them sit next to you. Encourage iteration often.  

## Engineers
The most successful product managers respect and have the respect of the engineers.  

Product managers should at least have taken an intro to computer science course. They need to understand engineering challenges to some degree.

With that being said, product managers should NOT tell engineers how to do their job. Give them the freedom to find the best solution given the constraints. Also communicate WHY they're building what they're building. Communicate customer problems and needs with evidence freely and often. Some engineers may not care, which is okay, but it's bad if NONE of the engineers care.
